Analysis description

Number of children who live to 5 (all)

In these models, we’re including everybody who was born. Those who die early, don’t marry/have kids are included with an offspring count of zero. Since so many died early in the historical populations, before getting a chance at reproduction, we have to model this process separately via zero-alteration. Hence, we use the MCMCglmm package which allows us to model this.

These models were run in parallel on our scientific compute cluster to check multiple chain convergence. The detailed model specifications can be accessed via the “Analysis script” links, the model output and diagnostic checks are in this document.

Raw data

To show the added value of mixed effects modelling, we first show a comparable raw data graph with bootstrapped means.

While the adjusted models serve to show that the association between paternal age and number of children is still visible when holding confounding variables constant, the raw data is important to understand what actually happens to the children of older fathers in reality (where confounders are not constant at all).

Let us assume that the reported association (Kong et al., 2014) between father’s age at conception and mutations holds up (and that we ignore the unknown father’s age at puberty which is presumed to be another important factor affecting the number of mutations). Consequently, this raw association reflects what we would expect to happen to the children who carry more de novo mutations.
