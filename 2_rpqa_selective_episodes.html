Analysis description

Data subset

The rpqa.1 dataset contains only those participants where paternal age is known and the birthdate is between 1630 and 1750.

Estimation

We fit all models using brms v. 0.9.1, a Bayesian regression analysis statistical package. brms uses Stan, a statistical programming environment to fit models using Hamiltonian Monte Carlo.

Covariates

As in our main models, we control for birth.cohort (birth years in five equally large bins), for male sex, for age at paternal and maternal loss, for maternal age (bins of 14-20, 20-35 and 35-50), for the number of older siblings (birth order), for the number of younger siblings, and for the number of younger siblings who where alive and dependent during the first 5 years of the child (defined as being alive simultaneously, less than 10 years old, and not dying in the first year).

Model stratification

We added random intercepts for each family (father-mother dyad). We then controlled for the average paternal age in the family. Hence, the paternal age effects in the plot are split into those between families and those within families or between siblings. The relevant effect for our hypothesized mechanism, de novo mutations, is paternal age between siblings.

Episodes of selection

Here, episodes of selection refers to survival, mating success, reproductive success. To clearly separate the episodes from one another, we make sure that each effect cannot be explained by the episode of selection preceding it. At its simplest, we check for effect on survival to reproduction only among those who lived to their first birthday. In later models, we look for an effect on number of children only among those who married and control their number of spouses.
